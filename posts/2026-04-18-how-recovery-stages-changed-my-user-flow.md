Once I decided to focus on injury recovery, it became clear that the application could not be designed like a generic fitness feed. A normal feed assumes users are browsing widely, following general interests, and discovering content in a loose way. That approach works for entertainment or broad inspiration, but it does not match the behaviour of someone trying to recover safely.

During recovery, users usually have a much more specific goal. They are not just looking for fitness content. They are trying to find information that fits their current condition, current level of pain, and current stage of progress. That means the flow needs to reduce noise instead of increasing it. Relevance becomes more important than volume.

This is why I started thinking about a stage-based flow rather than a general content feed. Instead of landing on a page full of mixed posts, users could first identify where they are in their recovery process. From there, the platform could guide them toward more relevant logs, discussions, and exercises. The flow becomes less about endless scrolling and more about helping users orient themselves.

This decision also affects the role of user-generated content. In a general fitness platform, posts often compete for attention. In a recovery-focused platform, the more important question is whether a post is useful to someone at a similar stage. Because of that, I think structured recovery logs could be more valuable than open-ended posting. If users can record their stage, symptoms, limitations, and small progress markers, then other users can compare experiences more meaningfully.

Designing the flow this way makes the platform feel more intentional. It shifts the focus from engagement for its own sake to guidance and decision support. That feels especially important in a recovery context, where users are often uncertain and trying to avoid making the wrong move.

At this point, I am still in an early design phase, but this direction has already changed how I think about navigation. Rather than designing for a broad audience with general interests, I am designing for users who need the interface to recognise context. That seems like a much stronger foundation for the rest of the project.
